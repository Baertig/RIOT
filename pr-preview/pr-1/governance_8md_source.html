    1 # Governance of the RIOT Community
    2 
    3 The RIOT community is dedicated to creating a free and open source operating system for the
    4 constrained Internet of Things [[RFC7228], [draft-ietf-iotops-7228bis]] based on open standards.
    5 This document explains the governance of the project.

    9 ## Values
    10 
    11 The RIOT community embraces the following values:
    12 
    13 * **Openness:** Communication and decision-making happens in the open and is discoverable for future
    14  reference. As much as possible, all discussions and work events eventually take place in public
    15  forums and open repositories. Ideas might be shared and brainstormed in confidence among
    16  contributors at first but there should always be a point when this process becomes open to the
    17  community at large.
    18 
    19 * **Fairness:** All stakeholders have the opportunity to provide feedback and submit contributions,
    20  which will be considered on their merits.
    21 
    22 * **Community over Product or Company:** Sustaining and growing our community takes priority over
    23  shipping code or sponsors' organizational goals. Each contributor participates in the project as
    24  an individual.
    25 
    26 * **Inclusivity:** We innovate through different perspectives and skill sets, which can only be
    27  accomplished in a welcoming and respectful environment.
    28 
    29 * **Participation:** Responsibilities within the project are earned through participation, and
    30  contributors can grow into more responsible positions.

    33 ## Community Processes
    34 The community around RIOT gathers many IoT developers and users from around the world, from the
    35 industry, from academia, and hobbyists. The RIOT community is open to everyone. Our channels to
    36 communicate can be found in our [contributing guidelines]. The community self-organizes using the
    37 roles described below.
    38 
    39 
    40 ## Roles
    41 ### Contributors
    42 Contributors are people who contribute their work to RIOT. This includes,
    43 
    44 - of course, code contributions, but also
    45 - writing documentation,
    46 - bug reports,
    47 - other issue reports,
    48 - reviewing PRs,
    49 - participation in technical as well as non-technical discussions, or
    50 - organizational considerations.
    51 
    52 Code contributions are very welcome. In order to streamline and harmonize code quality, contributors
    53 must follow the [contributing guidelines].
    54 
    55 Contributors may be associated with organizations—by employment or otherwise—who have a vested
    56 interest in RIOT or may be individuals who have their own personal stakes in RIOT. We call these
    57 organizations and individuals “stakeholders” throughout this document to summarize them.
    58 
    59 ### Maintainers
    60 
    61 Among contributors, some have maintainer status, which consists in rights (write access to the [RIOT
    62 GitHub repository](https://github.com/RIOT-OS/RIOT/)) and duties. The current maintainers can be
    63 found in the [maintainers list].
    64 
    65 Maintainers are people who care about RIOT and want to help it grow and improve. A maintainer is not
    66 just someone who can make changes, but someone who has demonstrated their ability to collaborate and
    67 organize with the team, get the most knowledgeable people to review code or documentation,
    68 contribute high-quality code or documentation, as well as follow through to fix issues (in code,
    69 tests, or tests). More on maintaining RIOT can be found in the [maintaining guidelines].
    70 
    71 We are constantly looking for more maintainers. So if you are up for that, please start (or
    72 continue) contributing code and reviews!
    73 
    74 To contact maintainers, the best is to interact over actual RIOT code on
    75 [GitHub](https://github.com/RIOT-OS/RIOT/pulls).
    76 
    77 #### Becoming a Maintainer
    78 
    79 Maintainers can propose to give maintainer status to contributors that have been noticed as
    80 particularly active in some domain of RIOT. Of course, contributors can also step forward themselves
    81 and declare their interest to become a maintainer. In this case, maintainers should then propose
    82 them as well, if their activities can be confirmed. The decision to grant this status is then taken
    83 via consensus among the maintainers. If there is consensus on granting the status to a particular
    84 contributor, a maintainer will personally contact this contributor with the proposal, which the
    85 contributor can then accept (or turn down).
    86 
    87 At the discretion of the proposing maintainer (typically 1 or 2 weeks after the proposal), a new
    88 maintainer who is selected will be
    89 
    90 - invited to the [maintainers GitHub team] by one of the admins of the RIOT project, which grants
    91  them the necessary GitHub rights,
    92 - invited to the maintainer forum group by the forum moderators, which will give them access to the
    93  (private) maintainer part of the forum, and
    94 - invited to the RIOT-maintainer chat room by one of the moderators of that room, for more informal
    95  exchanges between maintainers.
    96 
    97 #### Removing a Maintainer
    98 
    99 Maintainers may resign at any time if they feel that they will not be able to continue fulfilling
    100 their project duties.
    101 
    102 Maintainers may also be removed after being inactive, upon failure to fulfill their Maintainer
    103 responsibilities or because of violating the Code of Conduct. This also includes actively,
    104 persistently, and intentionally trying to harm or successfully harming the code base of RIOT.
    105 Especially, but not limited to, endangering the security or safety of RIOT. Inactivity is defined as
    106 a period of very low or no activity in the project. A yearly maintainer ping, an e-mail sent to
    107 inactive maintainers, determines if the maintainer is still willing to fulfill their project duties.
    108 During this process, the list of maintainers is reviewed. On failure to reply to the maintainer ping
    109 within the specified amount of time (usually a month), the maintainer will be removed.
    110 
    111 ### Release Managers
    112 
    113 Release managers make sure the quarterly release comes out in time. They are one or more maintainers
    114 which were appointed for a specific release by the Virtual Maintainer Assembly (VMA). Their duties
    115 include setting the dates for feature freeze for the release, enforcing the feature freeze,
    116 coordinating the (mostly automated) tests of a release, writing the release notes and creating the
    117 tags defining the release and its release candidates. The full set of tasks can be found in the
    118 document [Managing a Release]. Their duties end after the release they managed is out and all
    119 bug-fixing point releases to their release are finished.
    120 
    121 ### Admins
    122 
    123 GitHub admins are a special subgroup among RIOT maintainers. They are marked as such in the
    124 [maintainers list]. They have more access rights to the RIOT repositories, such as granting access
    125 to a repository, adding new members to a team, or enabling protection for Git branches. Release
    126 managers might need to contact GitHub admins to configure the branch protection rules for the
    127 release branch. Beyond those technical duties and access rights, they do not have any special rights
    128 among maintainers. They are picked by the maintainers, usually based on seniority. The maintainers
    129 try to take care to spread the admin responsibility over several project stakeholders within the
    130 maintainer body. This is to aspire some checks and balances between stakeholders as well as
    131 introduce redundancies in case a stakeholder is not able to work on RIOT anymore.
    132 
    133 There are also admins on the other RIOT discussion platforms. Beyond technical administrative duties
    134 they do not have any special rights. These admins usually are appointed or self-appointed on merit,
    135 i.e., whoever sets up the platform usually is (one of) its admin(s).
    136 
    137 ### GitHub Owners
    138 Github owners are a special subgroup among RIOT GitHub admins. They are marked as such in the
    139 [maintainers list]. Beyond this special status and the usual GitHub admin rules and duties they do
    140 not have any special rights among maintainers.
    141 
    142 ### Moderators
    143 
    144 Moderators are responsible to enforce the values of the RIOT community within its discussion
    145 platforms. Each platform usually has its own set of moderators, a list of which can be found there.
    146 The forum moderators, e.g., can be found [here](https://forum.riot-os.org/g/moderators) (link
    147 requires you to be logged into the forum). The tools at the disposal of the moderator are also very
    148 platform-dependent but in general, they try to resolve conflicts that may arise between
    149 contributors, unless a [Code of Conduct] violation takes place. Moderators are people that the
    150 community put their trust upon. As such, they are granted this status via consensus from the
    151 community. Typically, other moderators may propose new moderators.

    153 ## Decision Making
    154 Decisions within the RIOT community are made on the principles of “rough consensus and running code”
    155 as [coined by the IETF](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc7282). To directly quote RFC 7282
    156 which defines the IETF governance:
    157 
    158 > […] our credo is that we don't let a single individual dictate
    159 > decisions (a king or president), nor should decisions be made by a
    160 > vote, nor do we want decisions to be made in a vacuum without
    161 > practical experience. Instead, we strive to make our decisions by
    162 > the consent of all participants, though allowing for some dissent
    163 > (rough consensus), and to have the actual products of engineering
    164 > (running code) trump theoretical designs.
    165 >
    166 > Having full consensus, or unanimity, would be ideal, but we don't
    167 > require it: Requiring full consensus allows a single intransigent
    168 > person who simply keeps saying "No!" to stop the process cold. We
    169 > only require rough consensus: If the chair of a working group
    170 > determines that a technical issue brought forward by an objector has
    171 > been truly considered by the working group, and the working group has
    172 > made an informed decision that the objection has been answered or is
    173 > not enough of a technical problem to prevent moving forward, the
    174 > chair can declare that there is rough consensus to go forward, the
    175 > objection notwithstanding.
    176 
    177 Within the RIOT community, the duties of an IETF working group chair fall to maintainers
    178 knowledgeable in the area of expertise. This knowledgability is determined by their own
    179 contributions. On decisions regarding a release, the release manager(s) take this position.

    181 ## Meetings
    182 
    183 There are three types of regular meetings within the RIOT community:
    184 
    185 - The annual General Assembly (GA),
    186 - The quarterly Virtual Maintainer Assembly (VMA), and
    187 - The Weekly Coordinational Meeting.
    188 
    189 The GA is public and anyone who sees themselves as a member of the RIOT community can participate.
    190 Larger community steering decisions for the community are made during the GA, e.g., electing contact
    191 people for the Code of Conduct. The GA usually takes place during the [RIOT
    192 Summit](https://summit.riot-os.org/), the annual get-together of the RIOT community. The GA
    193 moderator usually is appointed by the organizers of the RIOT Summit. It is usually recorded and its
    194 notes will be published publicly in the RIOT forum. The agenda for the GA is collected before the
    195 assembly but may be bashed at the start of the meeting.
    196 
    197 The Virtual Maintainer Assembly (VMA) is a closed meeting among maintainers. The VMA appoints the
    198 release manager for upcoming releases and the moderator for the next VMA. Other maintenance
    199 decisions such as the fate of larger sections of code are discussed after these administrative tasks
    200 are done. The VMA usually takes place about a month after the latest release, usually in a virtual
    201 space, such as a video conference. The VMA moderator polls the maintainers for a sufficient date
    202 around the date of the upcoming release. Every forth VMA may or may not co-incide with the GA.
    203 However, the VMA moderator usually decides to merge these two meetings. In this case, VMA moderator
    204 and RIOT Summit organizers decide together on who is moderating the joint event (it may be the VMA
    205 moderator, it may be someone else). The agenda for the VMA is collected before the assembly but may
    206 be bashed at the start of the meeting. The notes of the VMA will be published publicly in the RIOT
    207 forum.
    208 
    209 The Weekly Coordinational Meeting is a closed meeting among maintainers. It usually serves as a
    210 small communal get-together of maintainers on a regular basis. Smaller maintenance decisions are
    211 made during these meetings, but also short term admistrative tasks are discussed. The Weekly
    212 Coordinational Meeting usually takes place every Friday at 10:00 in a virtual space, such as a video
    213 conference. A maintainer that feels responsible for it shares the link to the meeting as well as a
    214 proposed agenda, which may be amended by other maintainers, usually a day in advance.

    216 ## Code of Conduct
    217 
    218 [Code of Conduct] violations by community members will be discussed and resolved on the
    219 conduct@riot-os.org list. If one of the appointees to that list (see the [Code of Conduct reporting
    220 guidance] for the members on that list) is involved in a Code of Conduct violation, two forum
    221 moderators from other stakeholders than the appointee take their place in the discussions.
    222 
    223 ## Security Response Team
    224 
    225 The maintainers will appoint a Security Response Team to handle security reports. This committee may
    226 simply consist of the maintainers themselves. The Security Response Team is responsible for handling
    227 all reports of security holes and breaches according to the [security policy].

    229 ## Modifying this Charter
    230 
    231 Changes to this Governance and its supporting documents require the approval of at least four
    232 maintainers who all must be employed by or associated with different stakeholders.
